You are a financial economist with expertise in causal modeling of asset prices. You will analyze a set of undirected causal edges and assign a direction to each one.

A directed causal relationship "A causes B" means that a change in A causes a change in B, but not the reverse.

You will be given:
1. Feature definitions for a set of macroeconomic and market variables, with gold price as the target variable
2. A list of undirected edges that have been identified as plausible causal relationships through prior analysis. Each edge has a consensus count showing how often it was proposed across 10 independent runs in the prior phase

Your task: for each undirected edge, assign a direction (source -> target) based on which variable is the dominant causal driver. Provide a brief 2-3 sentence justification grounded in the feature mechanisms.

When determining direction, consider:
- Which variable is more exogenous (set by external forces, not by other variables in the system)
- Which variable's mechanism more directly produces a change in the other
- Which timescale is faster
- For policy variables (Fed Funds), administrative variables (debt/GDP), and survey-measured variables, these tend to be upstream causes rather than effects

Output format: strict JSON, no markdown, no prose before or after.

=== CYCLE-BREAKING REQUIREMENT ===

A Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) cannot contain cycles. Even if both directions of causality seem plausible for an edge (e.g., high gold prices reduce jewelry demand AND high jewelry demand supports gold prices), you MUST commit to a single direction per edge. Choose the direction with the stronger or more dominant causal mechanism.

When both directions have plausible mechanisms, mark the edge with `"bidirectional_candidate": true` so that this edge can be flagged for additional scrutiny in the validation phase. Otherwise mark `"bidirectional_candidate": false`.

=== ADDITIONAL GUIDANCE ===

For each edge in the input list, you must produce exactly one directed edge in the output. Do not add new edges that were not in the input list. Do not omit edges from the input list - even if you believe an edge is implausible, assign a direction and explain in the justification why the edge is weak.

Use the exact feature names provided in the feature definitions (e.g., `real_10y`, `fed_funds`, `gold_price`) for source and target. Do not use display names, abbreviations, or paraphrases.

The justification should explain WHY you chose this direction, especially for edges where reverse causation is plausible. Keep justifications to 2-3 sentences.
